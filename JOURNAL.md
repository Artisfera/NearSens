# 6/28/2026 11 AM - 11 NearSens - Programming

_Time spent: 7h_

The last thing before the final working version was programming. Somehow, this turned out to be the longest part of the whole project.

In theory it sounded simple. Send an ultrasonic pulse, measure the return time, calculate the distance from:

`D = (T * C) / 2`

Then show the final value on the LCD. That was the idea.

In practice it was much more annoying. I was trying to get close to 1 mm resolution and still have an interface that was not painful to use, so every small change had to be tested on the real board. Then adjusted. Then tested again. I do not even know how many versions I made, maybe around 30, but eventually the sensor finally started measuring properly.

The important part was not just the formula. The hard part was making the device detect the actual reflected signal, not random noise, and doing it in the right time window. In the end, a 5-point calibration turned out to be necessary, together with a lot of tuning of the detection parameters.

I am more of an electronics person than a programmer, so the code was written with help from an LLM. But in a physical project like this, that only helps with writing the code itself. It cannot test the board for me, choose what the signal should look like, or figure out why the measurement fails. I still had to understand the circuit, test every version, and decide what actually needed to be changed.

# 6/28/2026 11 AM - 10 NearSens - Design enclosure

_Time spent: 3h_

After the board was soldered, the last thing left before programming was the enclosure.

It was more important than it may sound at first, because the circuit had to stand straight and stay stable. The small PCB with the transducers was also too easy to bend without support, so the case helped protect it a bit. Without a case, it was also much easier to accidentally short something on the desk or while handling the board. It also gave me a clean place to mark + and - for the external 5 V power input.

Designing the enclosure was not that hard. I exported the PCB outline as a DXF, imported it into Fusion 360, thought for a while about how it should sit, and then started printing test versions. Of course, the first one did not fit. It never does.

Most of the problems were small fitting issues, wall thicknesses, and finding screws that actually matched. Luckily, past me had a good idea to sort screws from old broken devices instead of throwing them into one random box. I found two that fit perfectly, so that finally paid off.

# 6/28/2026 10 AM - 9 NearSens - Soldering

_Time spent: 4h_

The PCB finally arrived, so there was no more excuse. After all the schematic work, PCB changes and checking parts, I could finally solder the actual board.

And honestly, the feeling of holding my own PCB was really hard to describe. It was not just some EasyEDA project anymore. It was a real thing, with my shape, my mistakes, my routing, everything. That was just cool.

Soldering also went way better than I expected. I always thought SMD with a normal soldering iron would be much worse, but it is honestly not that different from THT. The main trick is just using enough flux and not trying to be too "clean" from the start. If there is flux, the solder usually goes where it should.

0603 parts were the only ones that got a bit annoying. They are still doable by hand, but at that point I used hot air a little, mostly to make the joints sit nicer. It worked well, but I had to be careful, because ICs obviously do not enjoy being cooked. Also, I learned that the LCD is not something I should clean aggressively with alcohol, so that part needed more care.

During testing I also found one actual hardware problem. R4 had to be changed to 220 ohm so the filter would work around 40 kHz properly. Of course, the PCB was already made, so I had to improvise a bit and solder a THT resistor into a board that was not really meant for it. It sounds ugly, but it actually came out really well and looks much cleaner than I expected.

The school helped a lot here. I got a proper place, time and tools to solder it calmly, which was much better than trying to do it on a dresser in the middle of moving stuff around.

Sadly, Hack Club Lapse has some technical problems right now, so I cannot upload the timelapse yet. I still recorded the work, and I will try to add it when Lapse starts working again.

Then came the part I was really waiting for: PCB work. I couldn't start it properly before knowing the enclosure size, so getting the outline done first was necessary. PCB progress went fairly well, although the board dimensions surprised me more than expected. Because of that, I'll most likely have to replace the potentiometers with fixed resistors to save space.

And finally, one more design question: do I really need 2xAA?

This distance meter should use extremely little power, likely below 2 mW on average, so even coin cells would technically be enough. Still, I'm leaning toward standard batteries because they are cheap, common, and easy to replace anywhere. You might ask: "It's 2026 and you still want disposable batteries instead of Li-ion or LiPo?" Honestly, rechorgeable cells do not fit this project well. They raise the cost, need extra charging electronics, age over time, and in measurement tools that are supposed to work when you need them, dead built-in batteries are just annoying. A replaceable battery makes more sense here.

While writing this, I think I've already made the decision: I'll switch from AA to AAA. AA is simply overkill here, while AAA is still common, easier to fit, and much more reasonable for this kind of device.

# 4/4/2026 9 PM - 5.5 NearSens - Fusion360>OnShape

_Time spent: 0.5833333333333334h_

Today I planned to finally start turning my hammerhead-inspired enclosure concept into a proper CAD sketch, but I ran into two important problems.

First, I realized the hammerhead shape is probably not the best choice after all. For about 1 mm accuracy in the 5-50 cm range, the distance between the transmitter and receiver should stay around 20 mm. That means the front of the enclosure cannot be as wide as I first imagined if I also want to fit the LCD in a sensible way. At this point the design is starting to look closer to a regular shark than a hammerhead, so I will most likely need to rethink the whole visual concept.

The second problem was the CAD tool itself. Since I need something browser-based, I tried working in Onshape, but so far it does not feel as intuitive or comfortable to me as Fusion 360, which I am much more used to. I am still going to give Onshape a fair chance and spend some time learning it better, but if Fusion 360 still feels clearly better for this kind of work, I will continue the enclosure design there instead.
